The risks of Digital Simulations and Serious Gaming

Digital Simulations and Serious Gaming for use of education can lead to risks which may hinder or alter results for projects, and also affect one's personal life.

As digital simulations and serious gaming improve throughout the years with educational learning, students and teachers begin to merge their work with digital systems and serious gaming to see how even with advantages and opportunities of its benefits, it allows risks to take place at the same time. As we see how some risks may affect the work of both students and teachers throughout the years as well, from designing, programming, costs, and limited resources, we see how even with great opportunities we see the risks balance it out.

Digital simulation and serious gaming are like double-edged swords that not only provide opportunities but also risks. Four significant risks have been explored and they will be discussed in this paragraph.

First of all, serious gaming can be used for the wrong purpose such as playing only just to win or gain points through the game. It is human nature that people feel a sense of rivalry, win, and feel superiority. However, according to Arata (2020), the addiction to victory can make them feel valuable themselves solely based on superiority over others. They believe that even in the use of good intentions using Serious gaming, many may take a negative approach to the use of it or may be oblivious to what to do with serious gaming. Furthermore, we see that it is important to identify specific educational issues that lack effective ways of teaching and guarantee these challenges can be perfectly converted into a serious game (Salomone, 2016). This idea shows why we need to review, improve, and be aware of what kind of serious games we offer students and whether serious games are being used by users in the intended direction effectively even after they are released.

Another risk is that the use of creating accurate 3D models can be challenging due to the complexities involved. Mistakes in programming and weak analyses used in the programmes can result in inaccurate models, misleading analysis, unrealistic behavior, user frustration, and hindered learning experiences. Thorough testing, prompt error resolution, and ongoing quality control are crucial to address these difficulties and ensure the production of high-quality 3D models. According to McDonald (2022), low-quality data used in algorithms can cause outcomes to be degraded and can also lead to repercussions based on those outcomes. McDonald (2022) stated that when the amount of data is decreased to 50%, the model starts to show degradation and when it decreases to 20%, the outcomes were significantly poor compared to the desired target feature, which shows that insufficient data can cause poor results. Therefore, in order to mitigate the potential consequences of failure, it is necessary to validate the simulation models.

Another important part of the risk is the high cost of digital simulators. The high cost of running multiple simulations can limit accessibility, strain computing resources, and pose data management challenges, impacting efficiency and effectiveness. From the perspective of students who have just begun learning, the pressure to acquire skills through expensive devices or to compensate for breaking them during training can also reduce motivation and concentration on their studies. Optimizing setups, prioritizing simulations, and adopting cost-effective computing solutions can help mitigate these disadvantages. Medical simulation software such as a human patient simulator varies in price, depending on factors such as the brand, features and services and these simulators may range between $10,000 to $100,000 (Costiuc, 2021).

Lastly, it is not recommended for young students spending for screen time watching and playing with digital screens. Young students can not only be addicted to serious games but also lights coming out through the screen can harm their eyes at an early age. According to Barnwell (2022), education providers should consider the age groups and what they are providing since it could bring more risks than benefits to implementing digital resources during classes such as reading. Uncontrolled screen time often causes difficulties in managing emotions and academic performances of students in both mathematics and language comprehension during their school years (Ray, 2022).

Overall, it is recommended to supervise and manage the use of digital simulations and serious games to carry out more benefits than risks.
